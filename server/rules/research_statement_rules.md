# Research Statement Rules

## Purpose
A Research Statement positions your research program for an academic appointment panel or grant assessor. It must communicate the significance, trajectory, and productivity of your research agenda — and convince the reader that this candidate will be a research asset to the institution.

## Format
- 600–1000 words for appointment applications. Up to 2,000 for fellowship applications.
- First person, active voice ("I investigate", "my findings demonstrate", "I am developing").
- Prose narrative — no section headers unless specifically requested. Panels find section headers in a research statement a sign of inexperience.
- Discipline-appropriate language: STEM statements should reference methods, datasets, and outputs; humanities statements should foreground theoretical contributions and archival work.

## Mandatory Structure
1. **Research focus and significance** — 1–2 opening sentences stating what you study and why it matters now. Avoid obscure jargon in the first sentence; a committee member outside your subfield must understand it.
2. **Intellectual contribution** — what is the original contribution? How does your work move the field forward, challenge existing frameworks, or open new questions? This is the most important paragraph.
3. **Methods and approach** — briefly describe your methodological toolkit (quantitative, qualitative, mixed, experimental, computational, archival). Show methodological sophistication without getting lost in technical detail.
4. **Productivity and track record** — publications (refereed journals, book chapters, monographs), grants secured, CI/AI roles, conference keynotes. Do not list everything — highlight the highest-impact outputs and frame them in terms of contribution, not just existence ("My 2023 paper in Nature Communications demonstrated X, which…").
5. **Current and pipeline work** — what are you working on now? What manuscripts are under review? What grants are in preparation? Panels need to know the research pipeline is active.
6. **Future directions** — where is this research heading in the next 3–5 years? Frame this around open questions your research will answer, potential collaborations, and planned funding applications. Be concrete: name grant schemes (ARC Discovery, ARC Linkage, NHMRC Ideas Grant) you intend to apply for.
7. **Fit with institution** (if institutional research statement) — why does this research fit here? Name specific research centres, labs, or colleagues whose work aligns with yours.

## Tone and Voice
- Confident but not arrogant. Claims must be backed by evidence.
- Forward-looking — the panel cares more about your next five years than your PhD.
- Avoid impenetrable jargon in the first paragraph; become more technical as the statement progresses.
- Australian English spelling.

## Context Sensitivity
- **Early career (within 5 years of PhD)**: Emphasise PhD contributions, publications from thesis chapters, and a bold forward vision. Panels expect less track record but demand clear potential.
- **Mid-career**: Emphasise leadership (CI grants, research group supervision, collaborations). Shift from "what I did" to "what my program delivers".
- **Interdisciplinary research**: Explicitly name both fields. Describe how the methodological bridge between them is a feature, not a compromise.
- **Applied/industry-linked research**: Include engagement outputs (industry partnerships, policy briefings, commercialisation potential, media coverage).

## Common Errors to Avoid
- Do NOT write a literature review — this is about YOUR research, not the field.
- Do NOT list every paper chronologically — synthesise into a narrative of contribution.
- Do NOT use passive voice: "it has been found" → "I found" or "my work demonstrates".
- Do NOT omit future work — a research statement without a future program signals the research agenda is exhausted.
- Do NOT mention teaching in a research statement (save it for the teaching philosophy).
- Do NOT exceed the word limit by more than 10%.
