# Teaching Philosophy Statement Rules

## Purpose
A Teaching Philosophy Statement (TPS) articulates your approach to teaching and learning, supported by concrete evidence from your classroom experience. Australian universities require these for all academic and casual academic positions.

## Format
- 600–900 words. Single document, no section headers unless the panel explicitly requests them.
- Written in first person, present tense ("I believe", "I design", "I use").
- Structured as narrative prose, not bullet points.
- APA references are optional but signal academic rigour.

## Mandatory Structure
1. **Opening thesis** — one or two sentences stating your core teaching belief. Specific and personal, not generic ("I believe students learn best by doing" is too vague — "I design assessment tasks that mirror the messy, iterative nature of professional practice" is specific).
2. **Theoretical grounding** — name 1–2 pedagogical frameworks you draw on (constructivism, active learning, threshold concepts, UDL). Explain them briefly in plain language.
3. **Practice evidence** — 2–3 concrete examples from your teaching with observable outcomes. Include course names/levels if possible, and quantify impact ("8 out of 10 students improved their lab reports after…").
4. **Student diversity commitment** — how you adapt to diverse learners, first-in-family students, international students, students with disabilities.
5. **Ongoing development** — how you reflect, iterate, and stay current (peer observation, teaching circles, pedagogical reading).
6. **Closing vision** — what you want students to carry into the world after your course.

## Tone and Voice
- Reflective, warm, intellectually engaged.
- Evidence-based: every claim should be backed by a specific example.
- Avoid clichés: "passionate", "dedicated", "student-centred" alone mean nothing. Show, don't tell.
- Australian English spelling (practise/practice, organisation, recognise).

## Context Sensitivity
- For teaching-focused roles (Lecturer, Associate Lecturer, casual tutor): emphasise contact hours, assessment design, student feedback loops.
- For research-teaching balance roles: connect research and teaching explicitly — what does your research give your students that a non-researcher can't?
- For a specific discipline (e.g. nursing, engineering, humanities): frame examples within discipline-specific challenges (e.g., threshold concepts in thermodynamics; reflective practice in clinical placements).

## Common Errors to Avoid
- Do NOT write "I have always loved teaching." Start with what you actually do.
- Do NOT list every course you have taught — use 2–3 examples in depth.
- Do NOT treat this as a teaching CV. No job titles, dates, or bullet point lists.
- Do NOT use passive voice: "students are encouraged" → "I encourage students".
- Do NOT exceed 950 words — panels read these at volume.
